Same prompt. Three weeks apart.

Week 1

Hi Sarah, I wanted to reach out regarding the spec document. We’ve hit a small delay; you’ll receive it on Friday. Apologies. Best, Chris

You edit: kill the “reach out”, drop the “Apologies”, change “Best” to “C”, change “Hi Sarah” to “Hey Sare”.

Week 2

Hey Sare, Spec doc is delayed by a day — you’ll have it Friday. Sorry for the wobble. C

You edit: tighten “is delayed by a day” to “slipped a day”. Add a question about her launch prep.

Week 3+

Hey Sare — slipped a day on the spec, you’ll have it Friday. Sorry for the wobble; legal review took longer than I’d hoped. How’s the launch prep? — C

You edit: nothing. Send.

What “learning” actually means.

  • →
    An edit becomes a rule after the third time. Cross out “just circling back” three times. PortaVox marks it as anti-pattern. Never written again.
  • →
    A nickname graduates after the second use. Swap “son” for “Manny” twice in messages to your wife. PortaVox locks it in — only with her.
  • →
    A relationship-specific style emerges after five edits. If you keep softening drafts to your investor, “more cautious tone with Marcus” becomes a contact-scoped override.
  • →
    Six writing slots fill in over time. Greetings, openers, body rhythm, asks, closings, sign-offs — each learns separately. You see them populate as your profile fills out.
  • →
    Reaction patterns library, not generic tones. Not just “friendly tone”. Specific: how you reply to good news, how you push back, how you decline an intro — learned from how you actually do it.
